Dried Blood Fertilizer: A Comprehensive Guide

Introduction

Dried blood, also known as blood meal, is an organic fertilizer made from the blood of animals, usually from slaughterhouses. It is rich in nitrogen and is often used to quickly increase nitrogen levels in the soil. This fertilizer is ideal for promoting leafy growth in plants and is commonly used in vegetable gardens and for lawns.

Best for Crops, Vegetables, Fruits, and Flowers

Dried blood fertilizer is particularly effective for plants that require a high nitrogen intake. It is commonly used for:

  • Crops: Corn, Wheat, Spinach, Lettuce
  • Vegetables: Leafy greens like Kale, Cabbage, Lettuce, Broccoli
  • Fruits: Grapes, Raspberries, Blackberries
  • Flowers: Lilies, Roses, Hydrangeas

Soil Condition Requirements

Dried blood fertilizer is suitable for all soil types, but it works best in well-draining, loamy soil. It should be used in soils with a pH level of 6.0 to 7.5 for optimal nutrient absorption. Overuse in sandy soils can lead to nitrogen leaching.

How to Use Dried Blood Fertilizer

  • Application Timing: Apply dried blood fertilizer in early spring before planting and mid-season for heavy-feeding plants.
  • Dosage:
    • For vegetables: Use 1-2 tablespoons per square foot of soil.
    • For fruit trees: Apply 1-2 cups per tree, spread evenly around the base.
    • For flowers: Use 1 tablespoon per square foot, especially for nitrogen-hungry flowers like roses.
  • Incorporation Methods:
    • Mix dried blood into the soil or garden beds before planting.
    • Sprinkle lightly around plants and water thoroughly to activate the fertilizer.
  • Watering and Activation: Water the soil immediately after applying to help the nitrogen seep into the ground.

Nutrient Composition

Dried blood is one of the highest nitrogen fertilizers available. Its nutrient composition typically includes:

  • Nitrogen (N): 12-15%
  • Phosphorus (P): 0-1%
  • Potassium (K): 0-1%
  • Organic Matter: High, improving soil texture and fertility

Benefits

  • Promotes vigorous leafy growth, making it ideal for leafy vegetables and lawns.
  • Quickly increases nitrogen levels in the soil, encouraging plant growth.
  • Enhances soil fertility by adding organic matter.
  • Helps improve soil structure and moisture retention.
  • Repels pests like deer, rabbits, and squirrels due to its smell.

Disadvantages

  • Overapplication can lead to nitrogen burn, damaging plants.
  • Strong odor may attract unwanted animals and pests.
  • It provides only nitrogen and lacks phosphorus and potassium.
  • Not ideal for plants that do not need much nitrogen, as it can inhibit flowering and fruiting.

Cautions

  • Avoid direct contact with plant roots, as excessive nitrogen can burn them.
  • Use gloves and a mask when handling dried blood to avoid inhalation or skin irritation.
  • Always follow recommended application rates to prevent nitrogen toxicity.
  • Store dried blood fertilizer in a cool, dry place away from pets and children.

Environmental Impact

Dried blood is a byproduct of the meat industry, making it a sustainable choice in terms of waste reduction. It is a natural, renewable source of nitrogen. However, its production process involves slaughterhouses, which raises concerns for those seeking fully plant-based fertilizers.

Impact on Soil Health

By increasing nitrogen levels, dried blood boosts microbial activity in the soil, which contributes to the breakdown of organic matter. This helps improve soil structure, aeration, and moisture retention.

Effectiveness for Plant Growth

Dried blood is particularly effective for promoting lush green growth in nitrogen-hungry plants. It is commonly used for lawns, leafy vegetables, and nitrogen-deficient crops.
